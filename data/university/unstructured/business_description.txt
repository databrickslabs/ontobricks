LAKEWOOD UNIVERSITY
Institutional Overview and Academic Management

---

1. Introduction and Institutional Profile

Lakewood University is a comprehensive research university founded in 1892, located in the Pacific Northwest. The institution serves approximately 3,500 undergraduate and graduate students across ten academic departments, with a faculty of roughly 60 full-time equivalent members. Lakewood is classified as a Carnegie R2 (Doctoral Universities – High Research Activity) institution and is accredited by the regional accrediting body.

The university’s mission centers on three pillars: excellence in teaching, advancement of knowledge through research, and service to the community and profession. To support this mission, the institution maintains integrated systems for student records, course management, faculty appointments, research administration, and grant management. This document describes the core business information model underlying these systems and the academic management challenges the university faces.

2. Academic Programs and Student Body

Lakewood offers bachelor’s, master’s, and doctoral degrees across the arts, sciences, engineering, and humanities. The ten academic departments are: Computer Science, Biology, Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Psychology, Economics, English, History, and Electrical Engineering. Each department is housed in a designated building (e.g., Engineering Hall, Science Building, Humanities Building) and operates under an annual budget allocated by the central administration.

The student information system maintains records for every enrolled student, including a unique student identifier, personal details (first name, last name, email, date of birth), enrollment year, declared major, cumulative grade point average (GPA), and current status (active, graduated, on leave, withdrawn). Students are assigned to a primary major within one of the ten departments, though interdisciplinary programs allow some flexibility. The university tracks student progress through course enrollments, which record the semester, year, grade, and status (enrolled, completed, withdrawn, in progress) for each course taken.

Academic management challenges in this area include: ensuring prerequisite chains are correctly enforced (e.g., CS201 requires CS101), managing course capacity and waitlists, supporting degree audit and graduation clearance, and providing accurate transcripts for employers and graduate schools. The course prerequisite relationship (which courses must be completed before others) is critical for curriculum design and student advising.

3. Faculty and Course Delivery

Faculty members are the core of Lakewood’s academic enterprise. Each faculty record includes a unique identifier, name, email, title (Professor, Associate Professor, Assistant Professor, Lecturer, Research Professor), tenure status (tenured, tenure track, non-tenure), hire date, and primary research area. Faculty may hold appointments in one or more departments through the department affiliation relationship, which records the role (Professor, Associate, Adjunct, Visiting, Emeritus), start date, and whether the affiliation is primary.

Course delivery is managed through the course assignment table, which links faculty to courses for a given semester and year. A faculty member may serve as instructor, co-instructor, teaching assistant, or grader. Courses are characterized by a course code (e.g., CS101, BIO201), name, credit hours, level (undergraduate, graduate, mixed), and maximum enrollment. Each course belongs to a department, which determines curriculum ownership and resource allocation.

Challenges in this domain include: balancing teaching loads across faculty, ensuring that qualified instructors are assigned to each course, managing adjunct and visiting appointments, and supporting faculty development and evaluation. The relationship between faculty, departments, and courses is many-to-many and must be carefully maintained for workload reporting and accreditation.

4. Research Initiatives and Funding

Research is a central component of Lakewood’s identity. Research projects are initiated and led by faculty, often in collaboration with colleagues from the same or other departments. Each project is associated with a department, has a name, start and end dates, status (active, completed, on hold, planning), total funding amount, and primary funding source (e.g., NSF, NIH, DOE, DARPA, NASA, private foundations).

Projects generate publications, which are recorded with title, journal, publication date, DOI, and citation count. Publications are linked to the research project that produced them, enabling the university to measure research output and impact. Grant awards represent external funding secured for projects. Each grant has a unique identifier, links to a project and a principal investigator (faculty), and records the grant name, funding agency, amount, start and end dates, and status (active, completed, pending, terminated).

Project collaboration captures the many-to-many relationship between faculty and research projects. Collaborators are assigned a role (Principal Investigator, Co-Investigator, Researcher, Postdoc, Graduate Assistant), a start date, and hours per week committed to the project. This structure supports effort reporting, conflict of interest disclosure, and recognition of contributions.

Challenges in research administration include: tracking grant lifecycles and reporting requirements, ensuring compliance with sponsor terms, managing cost sharing and indirect cost recovery, and maintaining accurate publication and citation metrics for promotion and tenure decisions.

5. Academic Management Challenges and Data Integration

Lakewood faces several cross-cutting challenges that require integrated views of the data described above.

First, student success initiatives require linking enrollment, grades, and course prerequisites to identify at-risk students and recommend interventions. A student transcript view that consolidates enrollments with course and grade information is essential for advising and degree audit.

Second, faculty workload and productivity reporting must combine teaching assignments, research projects, publications, grants, and department affiliations. A faculty profile view that aggregates these dimensions supports department chairs and deans in resource allocation and performance evaluation.

Third, research impact and funding analytics depend on joining projects, publications, grants, and collaborations. A research overview view that summarizes publications, citations, and grant funding by project supports strategic planning and institutional reporting.

Finally, the university must maintain referential integrity across all tables: students must reference valid courses, enrollments must reference valid students and courses, faculty must reference valid departments, and so on. Data quality checks and validation rules are critical to prevent orphaned records and inconsistent state.

6. Conclusion

This document has described the core business information model for Lakewood University’s academic and research operations. The model encompasses students, faculty, departments, courses, enrollments, course assignments, prerequisites, research projects, publications, grant awards, project collaborations, and department affiliations. Together, these entities and relationships support the full lifecycle of academic management—from student recruitment and degree completion to faculty hiring and research administration.

The views described—student transcript, faculty profile, and research overview—represent analytical perspectives that synthesize data from multiple tables to support decision-making. OntoBricks and similar semantic data integration tools can leverage this model to build a knowledge graph that captures the rich relationships between entities and enables advanced querying and reasoning across the university’s data landscape.
