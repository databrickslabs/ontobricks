1. COMPANY OVERVIEW AND MISSION
================================================================================

PharmaCorp Research Inc. is a mid-sized pharmaceutical company specializing in the development of novel therapeutics across multiple therapeutic areas, including oncology, cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, and neurology. Founded in 1995, the company has grown to manage an active portfolio of over 20 clinical trials at any given time, with sites across the United States and partnerships with major academic medical centers and community hospitals.

Our mission is to advance patient care through rigorous, ethical clinical research that generates high-quality evidence for regulatory approval and clinical practice. We conduct Phase 1 through Phase 4 trials, with a focus on patient safety, data integrity, and regulatory compliance at every stage of the drug development lifecycle.

2. CLINICAL TRIAL OPERATIONS
================================================================================

2.1 Trial Design and Execution

Clinical trials at PharmaCorp follow a structured lifecycle from protocol development through database lock and regulatory submission. Each trial is assigned a unique trial identifier and is characterized by its phase (Phase 1–4), therapeutic area, sponsor (which may be PharmaCorp or a partner organization), and target enrollment. Trials are conducted at multiple trial sites, each associated with a hospital or research institution. Each site has a designated principal investigator (PI responsible for the conduct of the trial at that location) and an enrollment target.

Patient enrollment is managed through a formal enrollment process. Patients are screened for eligibility, provide informed consent, and are assigned to a treatment arm (e.g., placebo, low dose, medium dose, high dose, or active control). The enrollment record tracks the status of each participant (screened, enrolled, active, completed, withdrawn, or lost to follow-up) and the dates of consent and enrollment. This structure supports enrollment tracking, retention analysis, and regulatory reporting.

2.2 Site and Investigator Management

Trial sites are the physical locations where clinical research is conducted. Each site is linked to a hospital or medical center and to a specific clinical trial. The site status (active, recruiting, closed, suspended) determines whether new patients can be enrolled. Principal investigators are physicians who hold the clinical and regulatory responsibility for the trial at their site. They are credentialed by the hospital and must meet sponsor and regulatory requirements for training, experience, and conflict of interest disclosure.

PharmaCorp maintains a physician registry that includes specialty, license number, and hospital affiliation. This supports site selection, investigator training, and audit readiness. Physician participation in multiple trials is tracked to support workload balancing and expertise matching.

2.3 Patient and Visit Management

Patients are the central participants in clinical trials. Each patient record includes demographic identifiers, contact information, and clinical attributes such as blood type and insurance identifier. Insurance information is used for billing and compliance with reimbursement requirements; it does not affect trial eligibility.

Patient participation is tracked through visits. Each visit is associated with an enrollment and has a visit type (e.g., screening, baseline, week 4, week 8, end of study, unscheduled, follow-up). Visit status (completed, missed, partial, scheduled) supports protocol adherence monitoring and data completeness reporting. Visit notes capture clinical observations and protocol deviations.

3. REGULATORY COMPLIANCE AND PATIENT SAFETY
================================================================================

3.1 Consent and Ethical Oversight

Informed consent is a cornerstone of ethical clinical research. All enrollments require a valid consent record. Consent types include initial informed consent, assent (for minors or incapable participants), reconsent (when protocol amendments affect participant rights), and withdrawal of consent. Each consent record includes the consent date, version, signatory (typically the patient), and witness. Version control ensures that participants are enrolled under the correct protocol version and that amendments are properly documented.

3.2 Adverse Event Monitoring

Patient safety is monitored through adverse event (AE) reporting. Every adverse event is linked to an enrollment and includes the event date, description, severity (mild, moderate, severe, life-threatening), outcome (recovered, recovering, ongoing, sequelae, fatal), and whether it is related to the investigational treatment. Events are reported by the treating physician or site staff. Aggregation of adverse events by trial, drug, and severity supports safety signal detection, regulatory reporting (e.g., DSMB, FDA), and risk-benefit assessment.

3.3 Lab Results and Clinical Data

Laboratory results are collected at visits to monitor safety and efficacy. Each lab result is linked to a visit and includes the test name, value, unit, normal range (low and high), and an abnormal flag. Abnormal values trigger follow-up and may lead to dose adjustments, protocol modifications, or adverse event reporting. Lab data supports pharmacokinetic analysis, safety monitoring, and efficacy endpoints.

4. DATA MANAGEMENT CHALLENGES
================================================================================

4.1 Fragmentation and Integration

Clinical data at PharmaCorp originates from multiple systems: electronic data capture (EDC), laboratory information systems (LIS), hospital systems, and regulatory databases. Patient identifiers, enrollment identifiers, and visit identifiers must be consistently mapped across systems to support integrated views. Today, many reports require manual joins and reconciliation. A unified data model that links patients, trials, enrollments, visits, lab results, adverse events, and consent would enable automated reporting and reduce errors.

4.2 Traceability and Auditability

Regulatory requirements demand full traceability of data from source to final analysis. This includes knowing which patient, enrollment, visit, and site each record belongs to, and when and by whom it was created or modified. Our current schema supports this through primary and foreign key relationships, but the proliferation of spreadsheets and ad-hoc exports increases the risk of orphaned or inconsistent data. Standardizing on a single source of truth with clear relationships and lineage would improve audit readiness.

4.3 Patient 360 and Cross-Trial Analysis

A patient may participate in multiple trials over time or have concurrent prescriptions and diagnoses from routine care. A unified patient 360 view would combine trial enrollments, prescriptions, diagnoses, medical procedures, and adverse events into a single profile. This supports safety analysis (e.g., drug-drug interactions and comorbidities), recruitment efficiency (identifying patients who may qualify for new trials), and real-world evidence integration.

4.4 Drug Safety and Pharmacovigilance

Drug safety requires correlating adverse events with drugs, both investigational and concomitant. Our prescription data captures medications prescribed by physicians, including drug name, dosage, frequency, and duration. Linking adverse events to trials and, where possible, to prescription data enables signal detection and regulatory reporting. A drug safety view that aggregates adverse events by drug, severity, and treatment-relatedness supports our pharmacovigilance obligations.

5. DATA MODEL SUMMARY
================================================================================

The PharmaCorp clinical trials data model comprises:

- Core entities: Patient, Physician, Hospital, Clinical Trial, Trial Site, Drug, Insurance Plan
- Transactional entities: Enrollment, Visit, Lab Result, Adverse Event, Prescription, Diagnosis, Medical Procedure, Consent

Relationships include:

- Patient → Enrollment (1:N)
- Clinical Trial → Trial Site (1:N)
- Trial Site → Hospital (N:1)
- Enrollment → Visit (1:N)
- Visit → Lab Result (1:N)
- Enrollment → Adverse Event (1:N)
- Patient → Prescription (1:N)
- Patient → Diagnosis (1:N)
- Patient → Medical Procedure (1:N)
- Enrollment → Consent (1:N)

This structure supports regulatory compliance, patient safety monitoring, and data management for OntoBricks ontology mapping and knowledge graph construction.
